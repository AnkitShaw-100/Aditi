China's Cognitive Warfare and India

Srikanth Kondapalli

Over the past few decades, nations have increasingly employed forms of warfare beyond traditional methods. As kinetic warfare has become costly and unpredictable, states have turned to non-kinetic methods that attempt to influence perception, morale, strategic narratives, media, and decision-making.

China adopted the "three warfares" framework in legal, media, and psychological domains and has refined it into a broader cognitive warfare approach. The purpose is to shape the adversary's mind before open conflict becomes necessary.

For India, this is not an abstract question. China's narratives around Galwan, border infrastructure, Arunachal Pradesh, democratic politics, economic capacity, and military partnerships all belong to a wider contest over strategic confidence.

Cognitive warfare works in the grey zone. It rarely announces itself as war, but it seeks to produce hesitation, distrust, and confusion inside the opposing state. India's answer must therefore combine narrative resilience, cyber preparedness, institutional coordination, and a whole-of-nation approach to strategic clarity.
