Inside the Government's North East Security Strategy

Brigadier Brijesh Dhiman (Retd.)

For over five decades, India's North East has been viewed primarily through the prism of insurgency. Armed separatist movements, ethnic militias, cross-border sanctuaries, and parallel political structures shaped public discourse and institutional security practice.

Contemporary realities suggest that this framing is increasingly inadequate. Organised insurgency-related activity has declined substantially, yet periodic turbulence and ethnic violence still reveal unresolved political grievances, governance deficits, and social fragmentation.

The strategic task is no longer limited to defeating insurgents. It lies in converting security gains into sustainable political legitimacy and social cohesion, while distinguishing between insurgency, hybrid threats, criminalised militancy, and ordinary law-and-order challenges.

Misclassification keeps regions trapped in emergency governance. A more precise approach allows civilian policing, judicial reform, development integration, and political settlement to carry the next stage of stability.
